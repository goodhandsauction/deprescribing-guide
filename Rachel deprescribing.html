🧠 The 5Ms Geriatric Framework (When to Deprescribe)

Mind

Cognition & Burden

• Worsening memory or confusion
• High anticholinergic burden (ACB ≥ 3)
• Unexplained sedation or delirium
Mobility

Falls & Gait

• Recurrent falls or unsteadiness
• Orthostatic hypotension symptoms
• Sedative-induced motor slowing
Medicines

Polypharmacy

• No active ongoing indication
• Prescribing cascade detected
• Inappropriate drug duplication
Multicomplexity

Frailty & Prognosis

• Severe clinical frailty index
• Time-to-benefit exceeds prognosis
• Significantly altered renal function
Matters Most

Patient Goals

• Expressed wish to stop drugs
• Swallowing or compliance issues
• Shift from preventive to comfort care

🔄 The 8-Step Deprescribing Protocol (How)

Compile full list of prescriptions, OTC, and complementary medicines. Verify indications and past ADRs. Cross-reference with My Health Record.
Explore patient beliefs and willingness. Reassure them that over 90% of older adults are open to stopping medications if recommended by a clinician.
Screen medications using STOPP/START v3, Beers criteria, or clinical guidelines where risks outweigh preventive benefit.
Look up the dedicated chapter module for the selected medicine or use the quick search tool above to fetch precise criteria.
Confirm if the protocol is an Evidence-Based Recommendation (EBR), Consensus-Based Recommendation (CBR), or Good Practice Statement (GPS).
Liaise with General Practitioners (GPs), nursing staff, and specialists to align on logistics, substitution options, and liquid/liquid-equivalent formulations.
Tailor recommendations to individual physiological clearance capacities, organ status, and the social context of the elderly patient.
Formulate a structured fractional taper schedule (e.g., 25-50% reductions). Define clear review timeframes and monitoring boundaries for return of symptoms.
